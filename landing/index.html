Why Colors Lie

Have you ever made a gradient from orange to blue and noticed a weird muddy brown in the middle? Or built a color palette where some steps look identical and others jump too much? That happens because most color tools use math that doesn't match how your eyes work. Here's the difference:

sRGB (the default)
This is what CSS, Figma, and most tools use behind the scenes. The middle of your gradient goes dark and muddy. Steps feel uneven because they are — the math doesn't account for human perception at all.
CIE Lab (the "standard")
The industry standard from 1976 — almost 50 years old. A big improvement over sRGB, but still creates brightness bumps and slight color shifts. It was designed before we had good data on how people actually see color differences.
Helmlab
Built by training on 64,000+ real human color judgments. Instead of guessing how people see, Helmlab was optimized to match actual observations. The result: steps that look even because they were designed to look even.

What Makes Helmlab Different? MetricSpace

Helmlab vs Oklab: What's Different MetricSpace

You might have heard of Oklab — it's the color space built into CSS. It's good for smooth gradients, but it was designed by hand, not trained on real human data. That means it can't reliably tell you "how different do these two colors look to a person?" Helmlab can, because it was literally built from human answers to that question.

Oklab is a fixed analytical transform (no fitted parameters). Helmlab is a 72-parameter model trained end-to-end on 3,813 human-judged color pairs. Same interpolation quality, fundamentally better distance metric.

Helmholtz–Kohlrausch correction

Here's something most designers don't know: a vivid red and a neutral gray can have the exact same measured brightness, but the red will always look lighter to your eyes. This is a well-known optical illusion called the Helmholtz–Kohlrausch effect. Some color appearance models (like CAM16) can account for this as a separate step, but no standard color space has it built in — you have to apply it yourself, and most people don't. Helmlab embeds it directly into the color space, so your lightness values are corrected automatically. See for yourself:

Embedded H-K: L += w·Cp·f(h), with w=0.389, p=0.849. Some CAMs (e.g., CAM16) can model H-K as a separate adjustment, but no standard color space embeds it into the forward transform. Helmlab bakes it in — saturated colors automatically get a lightness boost proportional to chroma, so L comparisons are correct without extra steps.

Full perceptual distance metric

When you ask "how different are these two colors?", simple tools just measure straight-line distance. But human vision is more nuanced than that. Helmlab's distance formula knows four things that other systems miss:

DE = ((dL/SL)² + wC·(da/SC)² + (db/SC)²)p/2 / (1 + c·DE), then post-power q. Pair-dependent SL, SC weights (CIEDE2000-style), Minkowski p=0.804, monotonic compression c=1.59.

Dark colors need more room SL weighting Two dark grays need a bigger lightness gap to look different than two light ones. Helmlab adjusts for this automatically. Dark & light pairs weighted differently
Vivid colors are pickier SC weighting Highly saturated colors (like pure red vs. pure orange) need finer distinction than muted ones. Helmlab handles this. Chromatic pairs get finer discrimination
Not all dimensions are equal Minkowski p=0.804 Your eyes don't weigh lightness, hue, and saturation equally. Helmlab uses a special formula tuned to match how you actually perceive combinations of changes. Sub-Euclidean aggregation fits human data
Big vs. small differences Monotonic compression When two colors are very far apart, your eyes stop caring about the exact gap. When they're close, tiny differences matter a lot. Helmlab models this too. Large differences compressed, small amplified

Frequently Asked Questions

What is a color space, and why does it matter?

A color space is a system for organizing colors as numbers. Your screen uses sRGB, which is great for displaying pixels but terrible for answering questions like "how different do these two colors look?" or "what's the midpoint between orange and blue?" Different color spaces give different answers to these questions — and most of them get it wrong. Helmlab was designed to get it right.

Why does Helmlab have two spaces?

Because measurement and generation are fundamentally different tasks. Measuring "how different do these colors look?" requires a complex pipeline with hue correction, H-K compensation, and chroma processing (MetricSpace, 72 parameters). But generating smooth gradients and palettes works best with a simple, fast transform (GenSpace, 21 parameters). Trying to do both with one space means compromising on both.

Think of it like photography: you use one lens for portraits and another for landscapes. Same camera, different tools for different jobs. GenSpace gives you the best gradients; MetricSpace gives you the best distance measurements.

How is Helmlab different from Oklab?

Oklab is a hand-designed color space that's built into CSS Color Level 4. It's good for smooth gradients, but it was never trained on human perception data. Its distance measurements are unreliable — it scores worse than even 50-year-old CIE Lab (STRESS 47.46 vs 42.80).

Helmlab's gradient() uses CIEDE2000 arc-length reparameterization for perfectly uniform steps (CV ≈ 0% on any color pair). Oklab's 25–40% CV comes from naive linear interpolation without reparameterization — the same technique could be applied to Oklab, but Helmlab ships it built-in. GenSpace's real advantage is 6× better hue accuracy (5.2° vs 30.1° RMS), meaning the gradient path passes through more natural intermediate colors. And Helmlab's MetricSpace does what Oklab can't: accurate perceptual distance (STRESS 23.30, trained on 3,813 human judgments) with embedded Helmholtz–Kohlrausch correction.

Is Helmlab free?

Yes. Helmlab is fully open-source under the MIT license. You can use it in personal projects, commercial products, and anything in between — no attribution required (though it's appreciated). The source code, research paper, and all training data references are publicly available.

Can I use it in production?

Yes. The JavaScript package is ~10KB gzipped with zero dependencies and ships with TypeScript types. The Python package uses NumPy for batch operations. Both have full test suites (337 tests combined) and are published on npm and PyPI.

Does it work with Tailwind / Figma / CSS?

Tailwind: Use semanticScale() to generate 50–950 scales, then export directly to Tailwind config format.

CSS: Export palette as CSS Custom Properties with one function call. Also available as a <script> tag from CDN for browser use.

Figma: No plugin yet, but you can generate palettes in code and paste hex values. A Figma plugin is on the roadmap.

What does "STRESS" mean in the benchmarks?

STRESS measures how well a color system predicts human perception. Researchers showed people pairs of colors and asked "how different do these look?" Then they tested each system's predictions against the human answers. Lower STRESS = the system agrees more with what people actually see. Helmlab's 23.30 means it's the closest to human perception of any system ever tested.

STRESS (Standardized Residual Sum of Squares) is the standard metric in color science for evaluating color difference formulas. It measures the correlation between predicted and observed color differences on a reference dataset (COMBVD = 3,813 pairs from 6 sub-datasets). Lower values indicate better agreement with visual data. Helmlab achieves 23.30, compared to CIEDE2000's 29.18 and CIE Lab's 42.80.

How was STRESS 23.30 actually measured?

The data: COMBVD is a combined visual-difference dataset of 3,813 color pairs compiled from 6 independent psychophysical experiments (Luo & Rigg 1986, RIT-DuPont, Witt, Leeds, BFD, and He et al. 2022). In each experiment, human observers viewed pairs of physical color samples under controlled D65 lighting and rated the perceived difference between them. The aggregate dataset contains over 64,000 individual human judgments.

The metric: STRESS is a standardized goodness-of-fit measure defined by the CIE (Commission Internationale de l'Éclairage). For each pair i, let ΔVi be the human-reported visual difference and ΔEi be the formula's predicted distance. STRESS finds the optimal scaling factor F that minimizes the residual, then computes:

STRESS = 100 × √( Σ(ΔEi − F·ΔVi)² / Σ(ΔEi)² )

The scale is 0–100: 0 = perfect prediction, 100 = no correlation. This is the same formula used by the CIE to evaluate CIEDE2000, CIE94, and every other color difference formula in the literature.

The evaluation: Helmlab's 72 parameters were optimized using L-BFGS-B with 8 random restarts on an 80/20 train-test split (seed=42). The reported STRESS of 23.30 is the full-dataset value. 5-fold cross-validation confirms generalization (mean CV STRESS ≈ 23.5). Bootstrap analysis (10,000 iterations) gives a 95% CI of [22.50, 23.93] for Helmlab vs [27.64, 30.84] for CIEDE2000 — zero overlap, p < 10−4.

Full details, ablation study, and reproducible code: arXiv:2602.23010

Why not just use CIEDE2000?

CIEDE2000 is a distance formula, not a color space. It takes two CIE Lab colors and computes a "perceptual distance" using hand-tuned corrections (SL, SC, SH, RT). It's good — but it was designed in 2001 with only 5 tunable parameters.

Helmlab is both a color space and a distance formula with 72 jointly optimized parameters. Its distance metric includes similar corrections (SL, SC, Minkowski exponent, compression) but fitted to data rather than hand-tuned. Even Helmlab's Euclidean distance alone (no special formula) beats CIEDE2000 at 26.28 vs 29.18.

Is there a peer-reviewed paper?

The full methodology, evaluation, and ablation study are published as an arXiv preprint: arXiv:2602.23010. The paper describes the 11-stage pipeline, optimization procedure, cross-validation results, and comparison with 8 baseline methods.
